Social Security Benefits Guide for Seniors (2026)

Social Security Retirement Benefits

Social Security retirement benefits are monthly payments based on your lifetime earnings record. To qualify, you generally need at least 40 credits (approximately 10 years of work). The amount you receive depends on your 35 highest-earning years, the age at which you begin collecting, and annual cost-of-living adjustments.

The Social Security Administration (SSA) calculates your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) — the benefit you would receive at your full retirement age. Your actual monthly benefit may be higher or lower than your PIA depending on when you choose to start collecting.

As of 2026, the average monthly Social Security retirement benefit is adjusted annually. The maximum benefit depends on your earnings history and claiming age. Workers who earned the maximum taxable income throughout their career and delay benefits to age 70 receive the highest possible monthly payment.

Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Your full retirement age is the age at which you are entitled to 100% of your Primary Insurance Amount. FRA depends on your birth year:

  • Born 1943–1954: FRA is 66
  • Born 1955: FRA is 66 and 2 months
  • Born 1956: FRA is 66 and 4 months
  • Born 1957: FRA is 66 and 6 months
  • Born 1958: FRA is 66 and 8 months
  • Born 1959: FRA is 66 and 10 months
  • Born 1960 or later: FRA is 67

Early claiming: You can start benefits as early as age 62, but your monthly payment will be permanently reduced. Claiming at 62 with an FRA of 67 results in approximately a 30% reduction in monthly benefits.

Delayed claiming: For every year you delay benefits past your FRA (up to age 70), you receive delayed retirement credits of 8% per year. This means your benefit at age 70 would be 124% of your PIA if your FRA is 67.

Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLA) for 2026

Each year, Social Security benefits are adjusted to keep pace with inflation through the Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA). The COLA is based on the percentage increase in the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and Clerical Workers (CPI-W) from the third quarter of the previous year to the third quarter of the current year.

The 2026 COLA is applied automatically to Social Security retirement, SSDI, and SSI payments beginning in January 2026. The SSA announces the annual COLA each October for the following year.

COLA increases are important because they help maintain the purchasing power of Social Security benefits over time. Without COLA adjustments, inflation would gradually erode the real value of monthly payments. Check ssa.gov for the official 2026 COLA percentage and how it affects your specific benefit amount.

When to Claim: A Strategic Decision

Deciding when to start collecting Social Security is one of the most important financial decisions you will make in retirement. There is no single "best" age — it depends on your health, financial needs, other income sources, and family situation.

Arguments for claiming early (age 62): You need the income now, you have health concerns that may limit your lifespan, you have been laid off and need income, or you want to retire early and have no other income source.

Arguments for waiting until FRA or later: You are still working and earning a good income, you are in good health and expect to live past your mid-80s, you want the highest possible monthly payment, you have a spouse who would benefit from a higher survivor benefit, or you want to minimize the impact of the earnings test.

Break-even analysis: The "break-even" age — where total lifetime benefits from delaying equal those from claiming early — is typically around age 80–82. If you live past the break-even age, delaying was the better financial choice.

Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)

SSDI provides monthly benefits to workers who become disabled and can no longer perform substantial gainful activity (SGA). To qualify, you must have worked long enough and recently enough to earn sufficient work credits, and you must have a medical condition that meets the SSA's definition of disability.

Eligibility: You generally need 40 work credits (20 earned in the last 10 years) if you are over 31. Younger workers need fewer credits. Your disability must be expected to last at least 12 months or result in death. The SSA uses a five-step evaluation process to determine disability.

Benefits: SSDI benefits are based on your average lifetime earnings before your disability began. There is a five-month waiting period from the onset of disability before benefits begin. After 24 months of receiving SSDI, you automatically qualify for Medicare.

For seniors approaching 65 who are on SSDI, benefits automatically convert to Social Security retirement benefits at full retirement age with no change in payment amount. For more about disability programs, see our disability benefits guide.

Supplemental Security Income (SSI)

SSI is a needs-based program that provides monthly cash payments to disabled, blind, or elderly individuals (65+) with very limited income and resources. Unlike SSDI, SSI does not require a work history — it is funded by general tax revenues, not Social Security taxes.

Eligibility: To qualify for SSI, you must be age 65 or older, blind, or disabled, and have limited income and resources. The federal resource limit is $2,000 for an individual and $3,000 for a couple. Not all income and resources are counted — the SSA has specific rules about what is excluded.

Federal benefit rate: The federal SSI payment amount is adjusted annually with COLA. Many states supplement the federal SSI payment with an additional state payment, increasing the total amount received.

Medicaid connection: In most states, SSI recipients are automatically eligible for Medicaid. This provides essential health coverage for low-income seniors. For details on Medicaid eligibility, see our Medicaid guides.

SSI recipients may also qualify for other assistance programs. See our low-income programs guide for additional resources.

SSDI vs. SSI: Key Differences

While both programs provide monthly benefits to people with disabilities, SSDI and SSI have fundamental differences:

  • Funding: SSDI is funded by payroll taxes (Social Security taxes). SSI is funded by general tax revenues.
  • Work history: SSDI requires sufficient work credits. SSI has no work history requirement.
  • Income/asset test: SSDI has no income or asset limit (though earnings can affect eligibility). SSI has strict income and resource limits.
  • Benefit amount: SSDI is based on your earnings record. SSI is a fixed federal rate (plus any state supplement).
  • Health coverage: SSDI leads to Medicare after 24 months. SSI typically leads to immediate Medicaid eligibility.
  • Age eligibility: SSDI is available to disabled workers of any age. SSI is available to disabled, blind, or elderly (65+) individuals.

Some individuals may qualify for both SSDI and SSI simultaneously (called "concurrent benefits") if their SSDI payment is below the SSI amount. For a full comparison, see our SSI vs. SSDI comparison guide.

Spousal & Survivor Benefits

Spousal benefits: If you are married (or were married for at least 10 years before divorce), you may be eligible for spousal benefits equal to up to 50% of your spouse's PIA at your FRA. You can receive the higher of your own benefit or the spousal benefit, but not both. Spousal benefits are available once the primary worker has filed for benefits.

Survivor benefits: When a Social Security recipient dies, their surviving spouse may be eligible for survivor benefits. A surviving spouse can receive up to 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit amount if claimed at their own FRA. Reduced survivor benefits are available as early as age 60 (or age 50 if disabled). A surviving spouse caring for a child under 16 can receive benefits regardless of age.

Strategic considerations: For married couples, coordinating claiming strategies can significantly increase total lifetime household benefits. This is an area where consulting with a financial advisor who specializes in Social Security planning can be especially valuable.

Working While Receiving Benefits

You can work while receiving Social Security benefits, but before reaching full retirement age, your benefits may be temporarily reduced based on your earnings:

Before FRA: In 2026, if you are under full retirement age for the entire year, $1 is deducted from your benefits for every $2 you earn above the annual earnings limit (set by SSA each year).

Year you reach FRA: In the months before your birthday month, $1 is deducted for every $3 you earn above a higher limit.

At FRA and beyond: There is no earnings test. You can earn any amount without reduction to your Social Security benefits.

Important: Benefits withheld due to the earnings test are not lost permanently. When you reach FRA, SSA recalculates your benefit to give you credit for the months benefits were withheld, resulting in a higher monthly payment going forward.

Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Depending on your total income, a portion of your Social Security benefits may be subject to federal income tax. The taxable percentage is based on your "combined income" (adjusted gross income + nontaxable interest + half of your Social Security benefits):

  • Individual filers: If combined income is between $25,000 and $34,000, up to 50% of benefits may be taxable. Above $34,000, up to 85% may be taxable.
  • Joint filers: If combined income is between $32,000 and $44,000, up to 50% of benefits may be taxable. Above $44,000, up to 85% may be taxable.

State taxes: Most states do not tax Social Security benefits, but some do. Check your state guide for details on state income tax treatment of Social Security.
