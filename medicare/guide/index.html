Medicare Overview

Medicare is a federal health insurance program administered by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS). It was established in 1965 and now serves over 65 million Americans. The program is divided into four distinct parts, each covering different aspects of healthcare. Understanding these parts is the first step toward choosing the right coverage.

Eligibility generally begins at age 65, but younger individuals with certain disabilities or end-stage renal disease (ESRD) may also qualify. If you are approaching eligibility, it is important to understand the differences between Original Medicare (Parts A and B) and Medicare Advantage (Part C), as well as supplemental options like Medigap and Part D.

For a detailed comparison of Medicare and Medicaid, see our Medicare vs. Medicaid comparison guide.

Part A – Hospital Insurance

Medicare Part A covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care (up to 100 days per benefit period), hospice care, and some home health services. Most people do not pay a monthly premium for Part A if they or their spouse paid Medicare taxes for at least 40 quarters (10 years).

Key costs for 2026: Part A has an annual deductible that applies to each benefit period. After meeting the deductible, you pay $0 for the first 60 days of an inpatient hospital stay. Days 61–90 require a daily coinsurance amount, and lifetime reserve days (up to 60 total) have a higher daily rate. These amounts are set annually by CMS.

Skilled nursing facility coverage requires a prior qualifying hospital stay of at least 3 consecutive days. Days 1–20 are fully covered; days 21–100 require a daily coinsurance. After day 100, you are responsible for all costs unless you have supplemental coverage.

Part B – Medical Insurance

Medicare Part B covers medically necessary services and preventive care. This includes doctor visits, outpatient care, durable medical equipment, mental health services, and preventive screenings like mammograms, colonoscopies, and flu shots.

Premiums: The standard Part B premium is set annually by CMS. Higher-income beneficiaries pay an additional Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA). There is an annual Part B deductible, after which you typically pay 20% of the Medicare-approved amount for most services.

Late enrollment penalty: If you do not sign up for Part B when you are first eligible and do not have qualifying employer coverage, you may face a permanent premium surcharge of 10% for each full 12-month period you could have had Part B but did not.

Part B does not cover most dental, vision, or hearing services, long-term care, or routine foot care. Many beneficiaries choose Medicare Advantage or Medigap plans to fill these gaps.

Part C – Medicare Advantage

Medicare Advantage (MA) plans are offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare. These plans combine Part A and Part B coverage into a single plan and often include Part D drug coverage, plus extras like dental, vision, hearing, and fitness benefits.

Plan types: The most common Medicare Advantage plan types are HMOs and PPOs. HMO plans generally require you to use in-network providers and get referrals for specialists. PPO plans offer more flexibility to see out-of-network providers at a higher cost. For a detailed comparison, see our HMO vs. PPO comparison guide.

Costs: Medicare Advantage plans have an annual out-of-pocket maximum, which Original Medicare does not. Monthly premiums vary by plan — some plans have $0 premiums beyond the standard Part B premium. However, copays and coinsurance for individual services may differ from Original Medicare.

Part D – Prescription Drug Coverage

Medicare Part D provides outpatient prescription drug coverage through private insurance plans approved by Medicare. You can get Part D coverage either as a standalone plan (paired with Original Medicare) or as part of a Medicare Advantage plan that includes drug coverage.

How coverage works: Part D plans use formularies (lists of covered drugs) organized into tiers. Generic drugs are typically on lower, cheaper tiers, while brand-name and specialty drugs are on higher tiers with greater cost-sharing. Each plan has its own formulary, so it is important to check whether your medications are covered before enrolling.

The coverage gap: In previous years, beneficiaries faced a "donut hole" in coverage. Legislative changes have significantly reduced or eliminated out-of-pocket costs in the coverage gap. For 2026, check the latest CMS guidelines for current thresholds and cost-sharing in the gap phase.

Medigap (Medicare Supplement Insurance)

Medigap policies are sold by private insurance companies to help cover costs that Original Medicare does not, including copayments, coinsurance, and deductibles. There are up to 10 standardized Medigap plan types (A, B, C, D, F, G, K, L, M, N), each offering a different level of coverage.

Important notes: Medigap policies work only with Original Medicare (Parts A and B), not with Medicare Advantage. If you have a Medigap policy, you cannot also have a Medicare Advantage plan. Medigap plans do not cover prescription drugs — you will need a separate Part D plan.

The best time to buy a Medigap policy is during your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period, which starts the month you turn 65 and are enrolled in Part B. During this window, insurers cannot deny you coverage or charge more due to health conditions.

Enrollment Periods & Deadlines

Medicare has several enrollment periods, and missing them can result in late enrollment penalties or gaps in coverage. Here are the key enrollment windows for 2026:

Initial Enrollment Period (IEP): A 7-month window around your 65th birthday — begins 3 months before the month you turn 65, includes your birthday month, and extends 3 months after. This is when you should sign up for Parts A and B.

General Enrollment Period (GEP): Runs January 1 through March 31 each year. If you missed your IEP, you can sign up during the GEP, but coverage does not start until July 1, and late penalties may apply.

Annual Open Enrollment Period (OEP): October 15 through December 7 each year. During this window, you can switch Medicare Advantage plans, switch from Original Medicare to Medicare Advantage (or vice versa), and join, switch, or drop a Part D plan. Changes take effect January 1.

Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period: January 1 through March 31. If you are already in a Medicare Advantage plan, you can switch to a different MA plan or return to Original Medicare and join a standalone Part D plan.

Special Enrollment Periods (SEPs): You may qualify for a SEP if you experience qualifying life events, such as moving, losing employer coverage, or qualifying for Medicaid.

Understanding Medicare Costs

Medicare costs vary depending on which parts and plans you choose. Here is a breakdown of what you may pay:

Premiums: Most people pay $0 for Part A. Part B has a standard monthly premium adjusted annually. Part D and Medicare Advantage premiums vary by plan. Higher-income beneficiaries pay IRMAA surcharges on Parts B and D.

Deductibles: Part A has a per-benefit-period deductible. Part B has an annual deductible. Part D plans have their own annual deductible (some plans waive it).

Copays and Coinsurance: After meeting deductibles, you typically pay 20% of the Medicare-approved amount under Part B. Medicare Advantage plans set their own copay and coinsurance amounts, which vary by service and provider network status.

Out-of-Pocket Maximum: Original Medicare has no out-of-pocket maximum, which is a key reason many beneficiaries add Medigap or choose Medicare Advantage (which does have an annual cap).

For help with costs, dual-eligible beneficiaries can have Medicaid cover many Medicare expenses. Learn more in our Medicare vs. Medicaid guide.
