Medicaid Overview for Seniors

Medicaid is the largest source of funding for long-term care in the United States. Unlike Medicare, which is a federal program with uniform rules, Medicaid is administered by each state with federal oversight, meaning eligibility requirements, covered services, and application processes vary widely from state to state.

For seniors, Medicaid covers a broad range of services including hospital and doctor visits, prescription drugs, nursing home care, home and community-based services, personal care assistance, and durable medical equipment. In many states, Medicaid also covers dental, vision, and hearing services that Medicare does not.

Understanding how Medicaid interacts with Medicare is essential for seniors who may qualify for both programs. Dual-eligible beneficiaries receive the most comprehensive coverage available through public programs. Learn more in our Medicare vs. Medicaid comparison guide.

Eligibility Requirements

Medicaid eligibility for seniors (age 65+) is determined by several factors including income, assets, state of residence, citizenship status, and care needs. Each state sets its own eligibility criteria within federal guidelines.

Age-based categories: Seniors 65 and older typically qualify under the "aged" category of Medicaid. Some states use the Supplemental Security Income (SSI) standard, automatically enrolling SSI recipients in Medicaid. Other states use their own income methodologies.

Disability-based eligibility: Seniors with disabilities may also qualify under disability criteria, regardless of age. This can include individuals receiving Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI). For more on disability benefits, see our disability benefits guide.

Citizenship and residency: Applicants must be U.S. citizens or qualifying legal residents and must be residents of the state where they are applying. There is no durational residency requirement — you can apply as soon as you move to a new state.

Income & Asset Limits

Medicaid uses both income and asset (resource) tests to determine eligibility for seniors. These limits vary significantly by state and by the type of Medicaid program (community-based vs. long-term care).

Income limits: Most states set income limits for seniors as a percentage of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL) or the SSI Federal Benefit Rate. Many states have a "medically needy" or "spend-down" pathway that allows seniors with income above the standard limit to qualify by deducting medical expenses from their countable income.

Asset limits: Most states limit countable assets to $2,000 for an individual (the SSI resource limit). However, certain assets are exempt, including your primary home (up to an equity limit), one vehicle, personal belongings, and burial funds. Married couples receive additional protections under the Community Spouse Resource Allowance (CSRA).

Look-back period: For long-term care Medicaid, most states impose a 60-month look-back period on asset transfers. Gifts or transfers made for less than fair market value during this period can result in a penalty period of ineligibility. Proper planning with a qualified elder law attorney is essential.

Dual Eligibility (Medicare + Medicaid)

Approximately 12 million Americans are "dual-eligible," meaning they qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid. For seniors, dual eligibility provides the most comprehensive health coverage available, combining Medicare's acute care benefits with Medicaid's long-term care and additional services.

Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs): Even if you do not qualify for full Medicaid, you may be eligible for programs that help pay Medicare costs:

  • Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB): Covers Part A and B premiums, deductibles, coinsurance, and copays.
  • Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB): Covers the Part B premium.
  • Qualifying Individual (QI): Covers the Part B premium (higher income limit than SLMB).
  • Qualified Disabled and Working Individuals (QDWI): Covers the Part A premium for certain disabled individuals.

Dual-eligible beneficiaries may also qualify for Extra Help (Low-Income Subsidy) to reduce Part D prescription drug costs. Learn more in our prescription assistance guide.

Expansion vs. Non-Expansion States

Under the Affordable Care Act (ACA), states were given the option to expand Medicaid eligibility to all adults with incomes up to 138% of the Federal Poverty Level. As of 2026, the majority of states have adopted Medicaid expansion.

In expansion states: Adults under 65 with incomes up to 138% FPL qualify regardless of disability or family status. For seniors 65+, eligibility is still determined under traditional Medicaid rules based on the aged/disabled category, but expansion states often have more generous benefits and higher provider participation rates.

In non-expansion states: There may be a "coverage gap" where adults earn too much for traditional Medicaid but too little for Marketplace subsidies. Seniors 65+ are generally unaffected by the coverage gap since they qualify for Medicare, but Medicaid eligibility criteria in non-expansion states tend to be more restrictive overall.

Waiver Programs & Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS)

Medicaid waiver programs allow states to provide long-term care services outside of institutional settings. These waivers are crucial for seniors who want to remain in their homes or communities rather than moving to a nursing home.

Section 1915(c) HCBS Waivers: The most common type, these waivers fund services like personal care assistance, adult day care, respite care for caregivers, home modifications, assistive technology, and care coordination. Eligibility typically requires that the individual needs a nursing home level of care.

Section 1115 Demonstration Waivers: Allow states to test innovative approaches to Medicaid coverage and delivery. Some states use these waivers to expand eligibility or provide unique services.

Waiting lists: Many HCBS waiver programs have waiting lists due to limited funding. Wait times vary by state and can range from months to years. Applying early is important even if you do not need services immediately.

Long-Term Care Medicaid

Medicaid is the primary payer for long-term care in the United States, covering approximately 62% of all nursing home residents. Long-term care Medicaid covers nursing home care, and through waiver programs, home and community-based services.

Eligibility for long-term care Medicaid: In addition to meeting income and asset requirements, applicants must demonstrate a medical need for long-term care, typically at a nursing home level of care. A functional assessment evaluates the applicant's ability to perform activities of daily living (ADLs).

Spousal protections: Federal law protects the spouse of a Medicaid applicant (the "community spouse") from impoverishment. The community spouse can keep a portion of the couple's assets (the Community Spouse Resource Allowance) and a minimum monthly income (the Minimum Monthly Maintenance Needs Allowance).

Estate recovery: After a Medicaid recipient passes away, the state may seek to recover costs from the recipient's estate, particularly for long-term care services. This can include placing a lien on the family home. Proper legal planning can help protect assets. See our long-term care planning guide and senior legal resources for more information.

How to Apply for Medicaid

The Medicaid application process varies by state, but generally involves the following steps:

  1. Gather documentation: You will need proof of identity, citizenship or immigration status, state residency, income (Social Security award letters, pension statements, bank statements), assets (bank accounts, investment statements, property deeds), and medical expenses.
  2. Choose your application method: Most states allow you to apply online through the state Medicaid agency website, in person at a local Department of Social Services or Medicaid office, by mail using a paper application, or by phone.
  3. Complete the application: Provide all required information and documentation. For long-term care Medicaid, additional forms and assessments may be required.
  4. Attend an interview: Some states require an eligibility interview, which may be conducted in person or by phone.
  5. Wait for determination: States have up to 45 days to process standard applications and 90 days for disability-based applications. You will receive a written notice of the determination.

Tip: Consider working with an elder law attorney or Medicaid planning specialist, especially if you need long-term care Medicaid and have assets to protect. Many offer free initial consultations.
