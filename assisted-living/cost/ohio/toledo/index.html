Who Qualifies for Assisted Living?

Assisted living communities serve seniors who need help with daily activities but do not require the round-the-clock skilled medical care provided in nursing homes. There is no single set of eligibility criteria — each facility sets its own admissions standards — but most share common requirements.

Typical Admission Criteria

  • Age: Most communities require residents to be at least 55 or 62, though requirements vary
  • Care needs: Residents typically need help with at least one or two Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) such as bathing, dressing, medication management, or mobility
  • Cognitive function: Standard assisted living generally requires residents to be oriented enough to follow emergency evacuation procedures. Residents with advanced dementia may need specialized memory care
  • Medical stability: Residents must be medically stable and not require 24-hour skilled nursing supervision. Conditions requiring ventilators, IV therapy, or complex wound care typically exceed what assisted living provides
  • Financial qualification: Facilities require proof that residents can afford the monthly cost, whether through private pay, long-term care insurance, Medicaid waivers, or VA benefits

Assessment Process

Before admission, most facilities conduct a comprehensive assessment that evaluates physical health, cognitive function, behavioral patterns, social needs, and care requirements. This assessment determines the appropriate level of care and monthly cost. Many facilities offer trial stays of one to four weeks so families can evaluate whether the community is a good fit.

When Assisted Living May Not Be Appropriate

Assisted living may not be suitable for individuals who need continuous skilled nursing care, those with severe behavioral issues that put others at risk, or individuals who are completely bedridden and require medical equipment. In these cases, a nursing home, memory care facility, or home-based skilled nursing may be a better option. Consulting with a geriatric care manager or the family physician can help determine the most appropriate care setting.
