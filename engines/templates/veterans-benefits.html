Veterans Benefits for Seniors in {{state}}

Senior veterans in {{state}} have access to both federal VA benefits and state-specific programs designed to support their health care, housing, and financial needs. Many veterans and their surviving spouses don't claim all the benefits they've earned.

Aid & Attendance (A&A) is an enhanced pension benefit for wartime veterans and surviving spouses who need help with daily activities or are housebound. This benefit can help pay for assisted living, home care, or nursing home care.

Who Qualifies

You may qualify if you are a wartime veteran (or surviving spouse) who needs help with daily activities like bathing, dressing, eating, or adjusting prosthetics, or who is blind or in a nursing home.

VA Pension Program

{{#if vaPension}}
{{vaPension.maxBenefit}} Maximum Annual Pension

{{{vaPension.description}}}

Eligibility: {{vaPension.eligibility}}

{{else}}

The VA Pension is a needs-based benefit for wartime veterans aged 65+ (or permanently disabled) with limited income. Unlike VA disability compensation, the pension doesn't require a service-connected disability.

Basic Requirements

  • Served at least 90 days of active duty with at least 1 day during a wartime period
  • Age 65 or older, or permanently and totally disabled
  • Income below VA pension limits
  • Net worth below limits (adjusted annually)

Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers (PCAFC)

Provides a monthly stipend, training, respite care, and health insurance (if eligible) to family members caring for veterans with serious injuries or illnesses.

Available to caregivers of veterans enrolled in VA health care. Offers peer support, skills training, telephone support, and referrals to resources.

Tips for Navigating the VA Benefits System

The VA benefits system can be complex, but understanding how to navigate it effectively can make a significant difference in the benefits you receive and how quickly you receive them.

Gather Your Documentation Early

Having complete documentation is the single most important factor in a successful VA claim. Gather your DD-214 (discharge papers), service medical records, VA medical records, private medical records supporting your claim, buddy statements from fellow service members, and any documentation of service-connected conditions. The more thorough your documentation, the faster your claim will be processed.

Work with an Accredited Representative

Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs) like the American Legion, VFW, DAV, and state veterans affairs offices provide free, accredited claims representatives who can help you navigate the process. These representatives understand VA procedures and can help ensure your claim is complete and properly submitted. Their assistance is free and can significantly improve outcomes.

Understand the Appeals Process

If your claim is denied or you receive a lower rating than expected, you have options. The Appeals Modernization Act provides three lanes: Supplemental Claim (submit new evidence), Higher-Level Review (request a senior reviewer), or Board Appeal (appeal to the Board of Veterans' Appeals). Each lane has different timelines and requirements — a VSO representative can advise which option is best for your situation.

Check for State-Level Benefits

In addition to federal VA benefits, most states offer additional benefits for veterans including property tax exemptions, state veterans homes, education benefits, employment preferences, and hunting/fishing license discounts. Contact your state Department of Veterans Affairs to learn about all available programs.
