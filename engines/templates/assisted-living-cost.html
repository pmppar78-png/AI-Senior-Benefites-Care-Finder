Private Pay / Personal Savings

The most common payment method. Families use savings, retirement accounts, Social Security income, pension benefits, and investments. Some facilities offer financial assistance or sliding scale fees.

Medicaid Waiver Programs

{{locationName}} may offer Medicaid waiver programs that help cover assisted living costs for eligible low-income seniors. Availability and waitlists vary. Contact {{locationName}}'s Medicaid office for current information.

Long-Term Care Insurance

If you purchased a long-term care insurance policy, it may cover part or all of assisted living costs. Check your policy for benefit triggers, daily/monthly limits, and elimination periods.

Veterans Benefits (Aid & Attendance)

Veterans and surviving spouses may qualify for VA Aid & Attendance benefits that can help pay for assisted living. This benefit can add significant monthly income.

Life Insurance Conversion

Some life insurance policies can be converted to long-term care benefit plans or sold through life settlements to fund assisted living costs.

  2. Apply for VA Aid & Attendance

    Veterans and surviving spouses may receive up to $2,431/month (2026 rates) through this benefit. See {{locationName}} veterans benefits →

  3. Consider shared rooms

    Semi-private rooms cost $300–$800 less per month than private rooms — saving $3,600–$9,600 annually.

  4. Negotiate rates directly

    Many facilities negotiate, especially for long-term commitments, off-peak move-ins, or when occupancy is below capacity. Ask about move-in specials and rate locks.

  5. Choose all-inclusive pricing

    Facilities with all-inclusive pricing may be more cost-effective than those charging separately for each service. Compare total costs, not just base rates.

  6. Use long-term care insurance

    If you have a policy, it may cover a significant portion of assisted living costs. Learn about LTC insurance in {{locationName}} →

Key Cost Factors in {{locationName}}

  • Location within the state: Urban areas typically cost 15–30% more than rural communities
  • Level of care needed: Basic care starts lower, but enhanced care for mobility or medical needs adds $500–$2,000/month
  • Room type: Private rooms cost $300–$800 more per month than shared rooms
  • Community amenities: Resort-style facilities with pools, salons, and restaurants charge premium rates
  • Memory care add-on: Dementia or Alzheimer's care adds 20–40% to the base cost

Who Qualifies for Assisted Living?

Assisted living communities serve seniors who need help with daily activities but do not require the round-the-clock skilled medical care provided in nursing homes. There is no single set of eligibility criteria — each facility sets its own admissions standards — but most share common requirements.

Typical Admission Criteria

  • Age: Most communities require residents to be at least 55 or 62, though requirements vary
  • Care needs: Residents typically need help with at least one or two Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) such as bathing, dressing, medication management, or mobility
  • Cognitive function: Standard assisted living generally requires residents to be oriented enough to follow emergency evacuation procedures. Residents with advanced dementia may need specialized memory care
  • Medical stability: Residents must be medically stable and not require 24-hour skilled nursing supervision. Conditions requiring ventilators, IV therapy, or complex wound care typically exceed what assisted living provides
  • Financial qualification: Facilities require proof that residents can afford the monthly cost, whether through private pay, long-term care insurance, Medicaid waivers, or VA benefits

Assessment Process

Before admission, most facilities conduct a comprehensive assessment that evaluates physical health, cognitive function, behavioral patterns, social needs, and care requirements. This assessment determines the appropriate level of care and monthly cost. Many facilities offer trial stays of one to four weeks so families can evaluate whether the community is a good fit.

When Assisted Living May Not Be Appropriate

Assisted living may not be suitable for individuals who need continuous skilled nursing care, those with severe behavioral issues that put others at risk, or individuals who are completely bedridden and require medical equipment. In these cases, a nursing home, memory care facility, or home-based skilled nursing may be a better option. Consulting with a geriatric care manager or the family physician can help determine the most appropriate care setting.
